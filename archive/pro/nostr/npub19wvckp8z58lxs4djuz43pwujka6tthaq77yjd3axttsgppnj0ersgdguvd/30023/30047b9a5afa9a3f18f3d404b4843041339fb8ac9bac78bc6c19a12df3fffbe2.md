# Digital Independence Day

Today, America celebrates its 250th birthday.

Two hundred and fifty years ago, a group of ordinary people signed a document declaring that political power should not be concentrated in the hands of a distant authority.

Whether you agree with every decision our nation has made since then is almost beside the point.

The Declaration of Independence was ultimately about one thing:

Who should be in control?

That question hasn't gone away.

It has simply taken on a different form.

## The Digital World

Today, much of our lives exist inside software.

Our identities, writing, photos, relationships; our memories.

Increasingly, they're all entrusted to platforms that we don't control.

If the platform changes, we adapt.

If the platform disappears, we scramble.

If the rules change, we usually don't have much say in the matter.

For the last year or so (ever since starting Continuum), I've talked about "digital sovereignty", but I have to admit something:  I think I was imprecise.

## What Sovereignty Really Means

When people hear the word *sovereignty*, they often think it means self-hosting everything.

Run your own servers.

Own every piece of hardware.

Never trust anyone else's infrastructure.

There's certainly value in that approach.

But I don't think that's actually the goal.

Earlier this week I migrated Continuum's archive from GitHub Pages to Cloudflare Pages.

Then I built the first version of a Media Library using Cloudflare R2.

A year ago, I might have worried that somehow I was compromising my principles.

Instead, I realized something.

The providers changed.

The architecture didn't.

That wasn't a failure of sovereignty.

It was evidence that the architecture was working exactly as intended.

## Architecture Matters More Than Infrastructure

Technology changes, companies will come and go.

(Anyone remember MySpace ?)

Hosting providers and protocols evolve.

None of that should require rebuilding the software itself.

If I decide next year that I want to move from Cloudflare R2 to Blossom, Amazon S3, or my own object storage, I should be able to do that, *easily*.

My Media Library shouldn't care, nor should my article editor; the author certainly shouldn't care.

Changing infrastructure should feel like changing a component, not rebuilding the house.

To me, that's a much more useful definition of software sovereignty.

## Independence

The founders didn't create a nation that would never change.

They created a framework that could survive change.

Good software should do the same.

Perhaps digital independence isn't about owning every server.

Perhaps it's about building systems where no server becomes indispensable.

Where your identity, content or media isn't trapped and 
where changing providers doesn't require starting over.

The more I work on Continuum, the more I think that's the real objective.

Not independence from technology.

Independence from dependency.
