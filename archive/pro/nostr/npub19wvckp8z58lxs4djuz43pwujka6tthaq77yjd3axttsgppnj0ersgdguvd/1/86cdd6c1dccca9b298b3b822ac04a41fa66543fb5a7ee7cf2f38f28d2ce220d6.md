6/26/2026 — Continuum Didn't Pivot. It Converged.

One thing clicked for me today.

Over the past year, I've sometimes struggled to explain exactly what Continuum is.

It's a Nostr client, yes...

It's a local-first sovereign workspace...

It's a digital artifact signer...

From the outside, it may even have looked like the project kept changing directions.

Looking back, I don't think that's what happened at all.

Continuum didn't pivot.

It converged.

Each new capability exposed a more general problem that needed to be solved.

The project began as a local-first place to write and manage notes and articles.

As I started publishing through Nostr, it naturally evolved into managing multiple Nostr identities.

Earlier this year I generalized identity management beyond Nostr keys to support other identity types such as SSH, GPG, and Bitcoin. From there I built tools around using those identities to sign and verify digital artifacts.

More recently, I began building Continuum Vault to manage legacy online accounts and digital identities.

Each step solved a real problem I encountered while building the previous one.

None of these ideas appeared in isolation.

Instead, they all pointed toward the same destination.

Today I think I finally found the right way to describe Continuum.

** Continuum is a local-first identity workspace. **

Identity is the center of gravity.

Notes, articles, documents, artifact signing, authentication, browser integration, publishing, archives, and digital relationships all naturally fit within that model.

Nostr remains an important part of Continuum, but it is no longer the thing that defines it.

It is one of the identity systems Continuum supports.

The architecture document I published today helped crystallize that realization.

For the first time, I feel like the individual pieces of Continuum are no longer separate ideas—they're becoming parts of a coherent whole.
