*Andrew G. Stanton — July 5, 2026*

I've been thinking about a question lately that I don't hear many people asking.

Not because it isn't important, but because most of us have simply accepted the way things are.

The question is this:  **How do we build digital lives that we actually own?**

Bitcoin taught me to think differently about money.

Building Continuum has caused me to think differently about everything else.

## It Started with Bitcoin

When I first learned about Bitcoin, one of the ideas that immediately resonated with me was a simple phrase:

**Not your keys, not your coins.**

At first it sounds almost cliché. Then you realize it's pointing at something much deeper.

If someone else controls your keys, they ultimately control your money.

That's true whether they're honest or dishonest. Whether they're a bank, an exchange, or a government.

The more I thought about it, the more I realized that ownership isn't really about access. Lots of people can give me access to something. Ownership is different. Ownership means I remain in control even if the service disappears tomorrow.

Eventually I started asking myself another question.

If I believe that about money... shouldn't I believe the same thing about everything else?

## I Looked at My Own Digital Life

I've spent more than thirty-five years writing software.

Over those years I've accumulated all the things you'd expect after a lifetime of building software: source code, articles, photographs, presentations, documents, notes, emails, and conversations.

Some of those ideas eventually became software. Others became articles. More recently, one became *Building Continuum*, and I'm beginning to see the outlines of several more books, including *The Bitcoin Chronicles*.

Looking back, all of that work represents a significant part of my life.

I started building Continuum because I wanted my digital life to have a home.

The more I thought about it, the more I realized I was applying two different standards.

When it came to money, I believed ownership mattered.

When it came to my own digital history, I had quietly accepted that someone else's service would always be there.

That didn't seem very consistent.

It eventually led me to a much simpler way of thinking about it.

**Your digital life should begin with you-not with a platform.**

## Nostr Changed the Conversation

Around the time I started thinking about these questions, I also discovered Nostr (and shortly after that within a few weeks started building Continuum)

For me, Nostr wasn't just another social network. In many ways it represented the same ideas that had attracted me to Bitcoin in the first place.

My identity wasn't owned by a company.

I wasn't locked into a single application.

I could choose different clients, publish to different relays, and take my identity with me.

That was a fundamentally different way of thinking about social media.

Nostr became the foundation for Continuum.

But as I spent more time using it, I also realized that Nostr and Continuum weren't trying to solve the same problem.

Nostr gives me a way to communicate with the world using an identity that I control.

Continuum is where I organize, preserve, and build on everything that identity creates.

I don't see those ideas competing with one another.

I think they complement each other.

Nostr is how I communicate with the world.

Continuum is how I remember.

## A Home Isn't the Same Thing as a Destination

None of this means I want to stop using the tools I already depend on.

Git is an incredible way to preserve source code.

Nostr gives me a protocol I can publish to without asking anyone's permission.

Google Drive is useful for sharing documents.

Each of these tools solves a different problem, and I expect I'll continue using all of them.

What changed for me was realizing that none of them was really **home.**

They're places where I publish, collaborate, share, or communicate.

That's different from having **one place where my own digital life comes together.**

I don't think those ideas compete with each other.

In fact, I think they complement each other.

## Ownership Doesn't Mean Isolation

Sometimes when people hear words like *sovereignty* or *ownership*, they imagine disconnecting from the internet or refusing to use modern services.

That's never been my goal.

If anything, I want to participate more.

I want to publish on Nostr, write more books and articles, share software, and contribute to communities that matter to me.

The difference is that I don't want any one of those places to become the **only** place my work exists.

If my writing begins with me, I can publish it **anywhere.**

If my history belongs to me, I can choose whatever tools make sense today—and different ones tomorrow.

Maybe that's really the idea I've been working toward.

Digital ownership isn't about replacing every platform or convincing everyone to use the same software.

It's about making sure **your digital life has a place that begins with you.**

Whether that's Continuum, another local-first application, or something that hasn't been built yet isn't really the point.

The point is that your writing, your conversations, your photographs, your relationships, and your history shouldn't exist only because someone else's service allows them to.

They should have a home that belongs to **you.**

From there, you can share them with the rest of the world.

## Continuum Changed My Thinking

One of the unexpected things about building Continuum is that it changed the kinds of questions I started asking.

At first I was focused on practical problems. How should identities be managed? Where should data be stored? How do you organize years of writing in a way that still makes sense five or ten years from now?

As those pieces started coming together, I realized they all pointed toward a much larger idea.

What would software look like if it started with the assumption that *your digital life belongs to you?*

That's a very different starting point.

If your digital life belongs to you, perhaps your computer should be the primary source of truth instead of a remote server.

Perhaps applications shouldn't own your data at all. Instead, they become different ways of interacting with data that already belongs to you.

Perhaps publishing isn't where your work begins. It's simply one of the many places your work can end up.

Those ideas didn't radically change what I was building.

They changed how I thought about *why* I was building it.

Once your digital life has a home, you start asking different questions. You're no longer thinking only about publishing or archiving. You begin wondering what you can learn from having all of that history available in one place.

## Thinking Beyond the Timeline

One idea that's been occupying my thoughts recently is something I've started calling **relationship memory**.

Today's social applications are very good at telling us what's happening right now.

Who's posting, what's trending, what's new.

That's valuable.

But it isn't the whole story.

Once I had years of my own writing and conversations available locally, I found myself asking questions that had nothing to do with today's timeline.

Who have I consistently interacted with over the past two years?

Which authors have quietly influenced my thinking?

What conversations eventually led to an article?

Which articles eventually became a book chapter?

Which people have I replied to dozens of times but somehow never followed?

Those aren't really search questions.

They're relationship questions.

Most social applications aren't designed to answer them because their job is to help us understand what's happening now.

I'm becoming increasingly interested in understanding what has mattered over time.

The more I thought about it, the more I realized my own history isn't really a timeline.

It's a web of relationships, ideas, conversations, and projects that have evolved together over time.

I think that's a much richer picture.

## AI Makes This Even More Interesting

There's a lot of excitement around AI right now and rightfully so.

But I sometimes wonder if we're asking the wrong questions.

People ask whether AI can search the web, summarize articles, or write code.

I'm increasingly interested in a different question.

**What if AI understood my own history instead of everyone else's?**

Imagine asking:

*"When did I first start thinking about digital identity?"*

*"Show me every article I've written that eventually became part of Building Continuum."*

*"Which conversations influenced this idea?"*

*"Who have I learned the most from over the past year?"*

Those aren't internet questions.

Those are personal questions.

And they require something that many of us don't yet have.

A durable archive of our own lives.

## Maybe We're Asking the Wrong Question

People occasionally ask me what Continuum is.

Sometimes I say it's a Nostr client.

Sometimes I say it's an archive.

Sometimes I describe it as a publishing platform.

Sometimes I say it's an identity manager.

None of those answers feels completely satisfying anymore.

Increasingly, I think Continuum is an attempt to answer a question.

**What would it look like if we actually owned our digital lives?**

I'm still discovering the answer myself.

Every feature I build seems to uncover another implication that I hadn't considered before.

Relationship memory.

Version history.

Offline access.

Permanent archives.

Portable identity.

Those weren't items on a product roadmap.

They were conclusions that emerged from asking the same question over and over again.

## We May Still Be Early

I sometimes wonder if this conversation is where Bitcoin was fifteen years ago.

Back then people asked,

*"Why would I want to hold my own money?"*

Today, millions of people understand why that matters.

Perhaps one day we'll ask an equally obvious question.

*"Why would I let someone else own the story of my life?"*

Maybe that day is still years away.

Maybe it's much closer than we think.

Either way, I suspect AI will accelerate the conversation rather than replace it.

The more valuable our digital history becomes, the more important ownership becomes as well.

## One Final Thought

I'm not convinced we need another giant platform that promises to replace all the others.

In fact, I hope we don't build one.

I'd rather see every person have their own continuum.

A place where their writing, conversations, photographs, projects, books, software, and relationships naturally belong together.

Then every application—whether it's Nostr, GitHub, YouTube, or something that hasn't been invented yet—simply becomes another place to publish.

That feels much healthier to me.

Bitcoin taught us to ask who controls our money.

Perhaps the next question is just as important.

**Who controls the story of our lives?**

I'm beginning to think that question may define the next decade of software.
