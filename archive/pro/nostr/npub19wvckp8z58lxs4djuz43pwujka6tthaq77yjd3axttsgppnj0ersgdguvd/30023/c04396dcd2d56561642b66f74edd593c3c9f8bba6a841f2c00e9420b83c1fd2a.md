## Overview

Continuum introduces a distinct approach to authorship by separating creation, finalization, and distribution into independent stages.

This enables a more precise and flexible model:

> draft → signed → published

Each stage represents a different level of authority and intent.

---

## 1. Draft

- Not signed  
- Editable  
- Not authoritative  

This is the working state of content.

Drafts can be modified freely and do not represent a finalized expression of the author.

---

## 2. Signed

- Cryptographically signed  
- Logically immutable  
- Exists locally  
- Not necessarily published  

This is the critical stage.

A signed artifact represents:

> a finalized expression of authorship, independent of distribution

Content at this stage is complete and attributable, but remains under local control.

It may never be published.

---

## 3. Published

- Signed and distributed  
- Sent to relays  
- Publicly visible  

Publishing is an outward action.

It does not define authorship, only distribution.

---

## Optional: Archived

- Organized and preserved over time  
- May be removed from active sets  
- Retained for historical continuity  

Archiving is a long-term concern and may be implemented as needed.

---

## Key Insight

Most systems collapse authorship and distribution into a single step:

> draft → publish

Continuum separates these concerns:

> draft → signed → publish

This allows:

- authorship without obligation to publish  
- publication without loss of control  
- clear distinction between creation and distribution  

---

## Implication

Not everything that is written needs to be published.

And not everything published defines authorship.

By introducing a signed, local-first state, Continuum enables:

> freedom expressed through software design
