One of the interesting things that happened today was completely unplanned.

I migrated Continuum's archive service from GitHub Pages to Cloudflare Pages.

It took maybe 1 or 2 hours (at most)

Not because I rewrote Continuum, or because I redesigned the archiving system

but, simply because I changed the deployment target.

The articles didn't change, nor did the archive structure

Only the infrastructure changed.

Why ?  

Because Github pages was running extra build scripts every time I committed an update
( and it causeed the pages to stop rendering even though all the content was uploaded properly to github)

That made me stop and think about what I've really been trying to build over the past year.

## What Does "Sovereign" Really Mean?

Sometimes people equate sovereignty with self-hosting.

Sometimes they equate it with decentralization.

Those are valuable goals.

But I don't think they're the whole story.

To me, sovereignty is about preserving choice.

If replacing one provider requires rewriting your software, you probably weren't very sovereign to begin with.

If changing infrastructure is simply changing an implementation detail, you've retained control.

## Providers Come And Go

Today I happen to be using:

* Cloudflare Pages
* Cloudflare R2

Tomorrow I may decide to:

* Self-host Blossom.
* Move media to Amazon S3.
* Run my own object storage.
* Deploy somewhere that doesn't even exist today.

None of those decisions should require redesigning Continuum.

The application should simply talk to a publishing media or archiving service.

The provider itself becomes interchangeable.

## Designing For Change

That's really what Continuum has always been about all along.

Not locking users into:

* one archiving service, one storage provider or one deployment platform

Instead, each of those becomes replaceable.

Ironically, today's migration strengthened my confidence in that philosophy.

I didn't have to rebuild Continuum.

I simply pointed it somewhere else.

That's exactly the kind of flexibility I want users to have.

## A Better Definition

Perhaps I've been using the wrong words.

Instead of saying:

> Own your data.

Maybe the better phrase is:

> Own your ability to move your data.

Or perhaps even:

> Own your namespace, not your provider.

Providers change.

Technology changes.

Companies come and go.

Architecture that preserves freedom to move—that's much harder to take away.

To me, that's what software sovereignty really means.
