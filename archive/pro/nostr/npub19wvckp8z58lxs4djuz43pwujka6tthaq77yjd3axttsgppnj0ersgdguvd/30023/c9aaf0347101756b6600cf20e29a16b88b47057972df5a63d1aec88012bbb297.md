---


Tonight was one of the most important conceptual sessions I've had since beginning Continuum.

For months I've struggled with a simple question:

> **"What problem does Continuum actually solve?"**

I've described it as:

- Local-first
- Nostr-based
- Identity management
- Publishing
- Relational memory
- Digital sovereignty

All of those are true.

None of them are the center.

## The breakthrough

I think I finally found the organizing principle:

> **Continuity.**

Technology changes faster than people do.

Applications change.

Protocols change.

AI systems change.

Companies come and go.

People remain.

Continuum exists to provide **coherence and continuity through technological change.**

Everything else supports that mission.

- Open source exists because continuity should survive the company itself.
- Local-first exists because continuity shouldn't depend on a cloud service.
- Nostr exists (today) because it provides a strong foundation for portable identity and publishing.
- Relational memory exists because continuity is about preserving context, not just data.
- Version history exists because our work evolves over time.
- Connectors exist because technology changes.

Suddenly all of these seemingly unrelated features make sense.

They are all supporting one idea.

## Nostr is not the destination

One of the biggest realizations tonight was that Continuum is much bigger than Nostr.

I love Nostr.

I still believe it is one of the most elegant decentralized identity and publishing protocols I've encountered.

But I no longer think it is the destination.

It is today's connector.

Tomorrow there may be another protocol.

If a better relay or synchronization protocol emerges in five years, Continuum users shouldn't have to rebuild their digital lives.

We update the connector.

Their identity, history, relationships, media and memory remain.

Protocols become adapters.

People remain the center.

## Identity above applications

Perhaps the biggest inversion is this:

Today, applications own identities.

Continuum wants identities to own applications.

Rather than creating another account for every new platform, I want a future where the platforms compete for access to *my* identity.

Google.

OpenAI.

Anthropic.

Future AI systems.

Future social protocols.

They become service providers.

The person remains the center.

## AI accelerates the problem

Another important realization:

The next generation of lock-in won't be files.

It will be memory.

Context.

Relationships.

AI assistants are becoming increasingly valuable because they remember us.

But if that memory lives entirely inside one vendor's ecosystem, switching providers becomes increasingly painful.

Continuum aims to become the stable foundation beneath those changing AI systems.

## The Phil Charter example

Ironically, later in the evening I saw a post from Phil Charter.

He had signed into a new phone using Amber.

Same Nostr identity.

Same keys.

Yet his follow list had gone from roughly 700 accounts down to 10.

Technically this may be a relay synchronization issue or a contact list problem.

From a user's perspective it is much simpler:

> **My relationships disappeared.**

That single screenshot illustrated the continuity problem better than any philosophical discussion.

It also reinforced an idea I'd been considering:

Maintaining historical snapshots of follow and follower relationships so Continuum can preserve and recover social continuity over time.

## A Shark Tank exercise

We also spent a long time role-playing a hypothetical Shark Tank pitch.

Surprisingly, the exercise helped clarify the vision.

The pitch eventually became:

> **Every major technology shift forces people to rebuild their digital lives.**
>
> **Continuum exists so they never have to start over again.**

Instead of describing features, the conversation focused on a much larger idea:

Technology changes.

People don't.

Continuity becomes more valuable as the pace of technological change accelerates.

Especially in an AI world.

## Looking ahead

Whether this vision ultimately succeeds is something the market will decide.

Today Continuum has exactly one active user: me.

That's reality.

But tonight helped me realize that I'm not trying to build another Nostr client.

I'm not trying to build another CMS.

I'm not trying to build another note-taking application.

I'm trying to build a foundation for a person's digital home that can survive changes in applications, protocols, AI systems and companies.

Whether or not that vision ultimately becomes a business, I finally feel like I can explain **why** Continuum exists.

And that feels like real progress.
